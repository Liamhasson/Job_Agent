Hi,

Avaloq's mission — democratising access to wealth management — is fundamentally a design problem. Complex financial systems, built for institutions, being made legible and trustworthy to a wider range of people. That's exactly the kind of challenge I want to be working on.

My strongest project is Pulse, a mobile fitness booking app where I ran the full design process: user interviews, affinity mapping, two rounds of moderated usability testing with six participants, and the iterations that followed. I applied WCAG AA standards throughout — not as a compliance exercise, but as a design constraint that made the product better. One finding I'm proud of: participants were interpreting the 'Injury Log' feature as automated medical advice. I renamed and reframed it as a 'Training Log' — a small change that resolved a trust issue and a liability concern simultaneously. That's the kind of detail that comes from actually putting a product in front of real people and listening carefully.

On Cyvore, a B2B cybersecurity platform, I tackled a different version of the same challenge: too much information, not enough structure. A 35-second scroll with no clear hierarchy, and users dropping off before they reached what they needed. I rebuilt the information architecture and introduced scroll-triggered micro-animations to guide attention through technically dense content — using motion as a design tool, not decoration. Presenting and defending those decisions to stakeholders, and iterating based on structured feedback, is how that project moved forward.

On the tools side: I work in Figma daily, I have experience building and maintaining component libraries, and I've studied Material Design thoroughly — it's in my workflow as a reference system even where I haven't yet shipped a product directly on top of it. I'm also genuinely AI-native in how I work, using Claude and other tools to move from rough idea to testable prototype significantly faster than traditional workflows allow.

What draws me to Avaloq specifically is that the complexity is real and the stakes are high. Designing for private banks and wealth managers means the users are experts, the information density is high, and getting clarity wrong has consequences. That's a harder, more interesting design problem than most — and it's one I'd like to grow into.

I'd love to show you the work. Best, Liam Hasson
