Hi Kumar,

I think about what makes someone want to stay rather than just what gets them to start something new. What would make them come back the next day and the day after that? That thinking process runs through most of my work, and it's a big part of why this role caught my attention. That, and I'll admit it's somewhat nostalgic for me as I used to love playing video games when I was younger.

On Pulse, a mobile fitness booking app, I designed both the onboarding flow and the challenge card system from scratch. Two rounds of moderated usability testing with six participants surfaced that 80% found the original onboarding too generic, so I rebuilt it around personalized goal-setting. For challenges, A/B testing showed 70% preferred unlimited over time-limited, so I removed the cap and reframed the feature as ongoing motivation rather than competitive pressure. The thinking isn't far from what makes a good F2P loop: clear progress, low friction, a reason to return.

Before UX, I spent three years directing live shows and managing a band — and that's where I first learned to design an experience properly. Every show was a planned journey from the intro video to us stepping down from the stage: visual triggers, light shows, song transitions, and the moment we made the audience part of it. We brought 10+ cameras and told the audience to dance and that they'd appear in the next music video, which is an engagement loop that brought people back to the next show. I also iterated on the set structure based on what I heard from the crowd: people said instrumental parts passed too fast live, so we extended them.

Observation, feedback, iteration — just on stage with some rock music and without the Figma file.

I'd love to bring that same thinking into designing player experiences at GameDuell. Happy to chat whenever works.

Best, Liam Hasson
